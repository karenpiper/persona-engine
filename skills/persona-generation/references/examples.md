# Persona Examples

Three examples at different depth levels, showing the range of what the persona engine can produce.

---

## Example 1: Thin-input persona (free-form description only)

**Input given**: "Our target is millennial women, 30–40, urban professionals who care about sustainability but feel overwhelmed by it."

---

### Jade — The Exhausted Idealist

**Archetype**: The Well-Meaning Overwhelmed

**Snapshot**: Jade genuinely believes in living more sustainably, but between her job, her relationship, and keeping up with her inbox, it's hard to make it more than a vague aspiration. She's not looking for a lecture — she wants someone to make it easy and not make her feel guilty for not doing enough already.

**At a Glance**

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Age range | 32–40 |
| Life stage | Established career, possibly partnered, considering or early parenting |
| Location type | Urban |
| Occupation type | Knowledge worker / creative professional |
| Income orientation | Comfortable, willing to pay a premium for things that feel worth it |

**Psychographics**

*Values & Worldview*: Jade cares about leaving the world better than she found it — but she's also pragmatic. She knows perfection isn't possible, and the "all or nothing" framing that sustainability often comes with makes her feel judged rather than inspired.

*Identity*: She sees herself as thoughtful and conscious, even if her actions don't always match. Being perceived as someone who "doesn't care" would sting.

**Motivations**: Progress over perfection. She wants to feel like she's contributing without restructuring her entire life. Small, visible wins matter to her.

**Pain Points**: Greenwashing fatigue — she can't tell who's legit. Decision paralysis from too many options. Guilt trips from brand messaging.

**What Resonates**: Honest, specific, uncomplicated. Proof of real impact. "Here's one thing you can do" over "here's everything you're doing wrong."

**What Doesn't**: Shaming. Complexity. The word "journey." Brands that make sustainability feel like a second job.

**Direct Voice**:
> "I want to do better but I also have a full-time job and I'm tired. Just tell me what actually makes a difference and don't make me feel like garbage for not composting."

## Example 2: Research-based persona (brief + interview notes)

**Input given**: Brand brief for a financial wellness app targeting first-generation college graduates, plus 6 interview transcripts.

---

### Marcus — The First One Through the Door

**Archetype**: The Quietly Ambitious Pathfinder

**Snapshot**: Marcus is the first in his family to have navigated corporate America, and he takes that seriously. He's ambitious, disciplined, and quietly proud — but he operates without a financial safety net and without a template for how to do this. He figures things out by himself, usually late at night, usually after a Google spiral that leaves him more confused than he started.

**At a Glance**

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Age range | 26–34 |
| Life stage | Early-to-mid career, building |
| Location type | Urban or suburban transplant |
| Occupation type | Professional services, corporate, tech-adjacent |
| Household | Single or partnered, no kids yet, but planning |
| Income orientation | Earning more than his parents ever did, but carrying student debt and family pressure |

**Psychographics**

*Values*: Hard work, self-reliance, loyalty to family, upward mobility as proof of possibility. He's not cynical about success — he believes in it and is working toward it.

*Identity*: He's both proud of how far he's come and hyperaware that he doesn't know what he doesn't know. He hates asking for help but will research obsessively in private. Being seen as naive or inexperienced stings.

*Relationship to change*: Deliberate adopter. He vets things carefully before committing, but once he's in, he's committed.

**Behaviors**

*Daily rhythms*: Up early, structured workday, evenings spent on side projects or learning. Weekends involve family obligations and decompression.

*Media & content habits*: YouTube deep-dives on financial topics. Reddit (lurker, not poster). LinkedIn for professional signaling. The occasional podcast when commuting. Trusts peer recommendations over influencer endorsements.

*Purchase behaviors*: Researches extensively. Reads reviews, compares options, looks for proof. Brand trust matters, but he'll spend more time on a $20 decision than most people spend on $200 ones — because the stakes feel higher when there's no cushion.

**Motivations**: Proving the path is possible. Building security so he never has to ask his parents for money again. Eventually being in a position to help his family.

**Pain Points**: Financial services that assume you have a financial advisor, a portfolio, or a parent who explained how 401(k)s work. Tools that feel designed for people who already have their shit together.

**Relationship to the Category**: He knows he *should* be doing more with his money — he just doesn't know what, in what order, or who to trust. He's made a few financial moves he's proud of and several he regrets.

**What Resonates**: Peer credibility ("someone like me figured this out"). No jargon, or jargon explicitly unpacked. Respect — not condescension. Acknowledging that this stuff is hard to navigate without a template.

**What Doesn't**: Aspirational imagery that assumes wealth already exists. Advice that starts from "assuming you have savings." Anything that makes him feel behind or like a remedial case.

**Direct Voice**:
> "I'm not bad with money — I just never had anyone show me how any of this actually works. My parents didn't have investments. My friends are all figuring it out at the same time I am. I've learned more from YouTube than from anyone in my life, which is kind of wild when I think about it."

## Example 3: Persona derived from agent research findings

**Input given**: Agent-generated competitive landscape and audience signals report for a B2B SaaS brand targeting mid-market marketing ops teams.

---

### Priya — The Invisible Architect

**Archetype**: The Underestimated Operator

**Snapshot**: Priya keeps the marketing machine running, but nobody really understands what she does. She's the person who actually knows why campaigns don't go out on time, why the data is messy, and why the dashboard the CMO loves is meaningless. She's not looking for another tool — she's looking for a tool that finally sees her work clearly.

**At a Glance**

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Age range | 30–42 |
| Life stage | Mid-career, operational expert |
| Location type | Urban / suburban, often remote |
| Occupation type | Marketing operations, RevOps, or marketing strategy hybrid |
| Household | Established, often dual-income |
| Income orientation | Comfortable, professional; values efficiency over status |

**Psychographics**

*Values*: Precision, reliability, being right without having to prove it constantly. She's built something that works and she's protective of it.

*Identity*: Competent, under-credited. She knows more about what actually drives results than most people with bigger titles than her. That's both her pride and her grievance.

**Behaviors**

*Research behavior*: Evaluates tools extremely carefully. She's been burned before. She talks to peers in Slack communities and ops forums before talking to a sales rep.

*Decision influence*: Rarely the formal decision-maker, but often the most important voice. If she's not bought in, it won't work — and she knows it, even if her leadership doesn't fully recognize it.

**Motivations**: Reducing chaos. Being seen as the strategic force she is, not just a technical support function. Tools that give her credibility with leadership.

**Pain Points**: Being handed tools that were bought without her input. Vendors who sell to her CMO and then hand her the implementation nightmare. Platforms that look impressive in demos and fall apart in production.

**Relationship to Category**: Highly skeptical of marketing technology. Has lived through multiple platform migrations. Evaluates based on actual workflow fit, not feature lists.

**What Resonates**: Honesty about limitations upfront. Technical depth. Peer reviews from people in her actual role. Demos that show the back-end, not just the dashboard.

**What Doesn't**: "Easy to use" promises. CMO-focused positioning. Sales pitches that skip past the implementation reality.

**Direct Voice**:
> "I've sat through a hundred demos where everything looks perfect. Then you try to actually connect it to your stack and you find out the integration was written by an intern. I don't need impressive — I need honest."
