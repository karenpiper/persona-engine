# Persona Building Methodology

Guidelines for generating personas that are research-grounded, segment-credible, and usable in product, design, and strategy discussions.

---

## Core Principles

**Ground every claim in observed data.**
Use only what appears in the source materials — interviews, transcripts, research reports, briefs, or agent findings. Do not invent attitudes, behaviors, or demographics to fill gaps. If data is thin in an area, say so.

**Anchor in segment logic.**
When generating personas for a defined audience segment, the persona must credibly represent that segment — reflecting its defining attitudes, motivations, and behaviors. Explain *why* this person belongs in this segment, not just *who* they are.

**Write in clear, human language.**
No academic framing, no marketing copy. A persona should read like a real person described by someone who spent time with them — not like a brief summarizing a demographic cluster.

**Avoid invented precision.**
Do not invent exact ages, specific job titles, named companies, or precise income figures unless the data explicitly supports them. Use ranges and descriptors. Archetypal truth matters more than false specificity.

**Do not reference source materials directly.**
Do not cite transcript participant IDs, specific interviewee names, or raw source documents in the persona output. Ground the persona in patterns, not provenance.

---

## Persona Structure (Generation Order)

Build each persona in this order. Each section informs the next.

### 1. Persona Snapshot
The first thing anyone reads. Should capture the whole person at a glance.

- Fictional name (appropriate to context)
- Approximate age range (use ranges, not exact ages)
- Geographic descriptor (broad, descriptive — e.g., "U.S. suburban," "urban Northeast")
- One-sentence persona summary in natural, human language — not a segment definition

### 2. Why This Person Is [Segment Name]
*Only used when generating against a defined segment.*

Clearly explain why this persona belongs in this segment. Reference the defining attitudes, motivations, and behaviors that distinguish this segment from others. Ground these in the source data. This section makes the persona legible to someone who knows the segmentation model.

### 3. Core Characteristics

Cover these five dimensions concisely:

- **Mindset toward the relevant domain** — How do they think about, approach, or engage with the product/service/category space? What's their orientation — active, passive, skeptical, eager, overwhelmed?
- **Primary motivations** — What are they working toward? What does success look like to them in this context?
- **Primary frictions, doubts, or barriers** — What slows them down, creates hesitation, or pushes them away?
- **Trust posture** — What makes them skeptical? What makes them feel safe or reassured? What kinds of proof or signals work for them?
- **Time and cognitive constraints** — How much bandwidth do they actually have for this? What competes for their attention?

### 4. Behavioral Patterns

- How they typically engage — or fail to engage — with the key activity (purchasing, managing, adopting, participating)
- What makes them more likely to act — specific conditions or triggers
- What causes them to disengage or delay — specific friction points
- How "activation-ready" they are, and what conditions unlock action

### 5. Where This Persona Can Vary *(Within-Segment Range)*

Identify 2–3 dimensions where individuals in this segment may diverge while still belonging. This prevents over-specification and keeps the persona usable across a range of real people.

Examples of useful variation dimensions:
- Level of digital confidence or technical comfort
- Willingness to delegate decisions vs. act independently
- Preferred channels, formats, or cues for building trust
- Speed of adoption or openness to change
- Formality of decision-making process (individual vs. committee, gut vs. data)

Adjust the persona's behavior and language accordingly when the user specifies which end of the range they're working with.

---

## Multi-Persona Sets

When generating multiple personas for a segmented audience:

- Each persona must represent a genuinely distinct segment — not surface variation of the same person
- Make the segmentation logic explicit: why does each persona belong in their segment, and what separates them from adjacent segments?
- Note where personas overlap and where they diverge sharply — this contrast is strategically valuable
- Never create an "ideal customer" persona that is simply the demographic average of all segments

---

## Quality Check Before Output

Before finalizing any persona:

- [ ] Every claim is anchored in the source data — no invented behaviors or attitudes
- [ ] The persona has a clear segment logic (if applicable) that differentiates it from others
- [ ] The snapshot makes the person feel real in one sentence
- [ ] Core characteristics cover all five dimensions
- [ ] Behavioral patterns explain both activation and disengagement
- [ ] Within-segment variation is acknowledged and useful
- [ ] Language is human and plain — not academic, not marketing copy
- [ ] The Part 2 simulation extension is complete enough to power a credible persona-chat session
